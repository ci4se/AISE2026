About the First Edition

A dynamic AI landscape is emerging throughout Africa, poised to have a transformative impact. In Africa alone, over 2,400 companies are reportedly involved in AI. According to Statista, the generative AI market in Africa is expected to expand from $0.89 billion in 2024 to $8.75 billion by 2028. More than 30 AI communities — from developing regions such as Sudan and Mali to well-established centers like South Africa and Tunisia — are leading this movement.

Against this backdrop, the first edition of the IEEE CIS Summer School on Computational Intelligence for Software Engineering brought together senior undergraduate, M.Sc., and Ph.D. students from the region and beyond. The school provided hands-on knowledge and expert insights into future developments at the intersection of AI and software engineering, covering the design of AI-enabled systems, their evolution, and debugging.

Held at IHEC Carthage over three days, the event featured plenary talks from active researchers at North American and international institutes, complemented by lectures from local academics and industry practitioners. The school was co-sponsored by the LARODEC Laboratory and the Institute of Data Engineering, Analytics, and Science at the University of Michigan–Flint.

Impact & Benefits

The IEEE CIS AISE 2025 Summer School aimed to engage young researchers, as well as graduate and postgraduate students worldwide with an interest in this domain. The program specifically sought to increase participation from Africa, addressing the underrepresentation of African contributions in leading conferences on Artificial Intelligence and Software Engineering. Consequently, it was essential to establish connections between African researchers and prominent experts to facilitate learning and collaborative opportunities.

Participants benefited from comprehensive educational sessions delivered by renowned specialists, keeping them informed of the latest advancements and trends while equipping them with practical research skills. The summer school fostered an interactive environment where attendees exchanged insights and learned from one another, enhancing their understanding and preparing them to tackle the emerging challenges associated with integrating Large Language Models (LLMs) into existing software systems.
